Introduction to Matter

The foundational philosophy and scientific definition of everything that surrounds us in the universe.

What is Matter?

Look around your surroundings. You see a large variety of things with different shapes, sizes, and textures. Everything in this universe is made up of material which scientists have named "matter". The air we breathe, the food we eat, stones, clouds, stars, plants, and animals, even a small drop of water or a particle of sand – everything is matter.

The two fundamental conditions for anything to be classified as matter are:
1. It must have Mass.
2. It must occupy Space (Volume).

Historical Context (Panch Tatva)

Early Indian philosophers classified matter in the form of five basic elements – the "Panch Tatva": Air, Earth, Fire, Sky, and Water. According to them, everything, living or non-living, was made up of these five basic elements. Ancient Greek philosophers had arrived at a similar classification of matter.

Modern-day scientists have evolved two types of classification of matter based on their physical properties and chemical nature. In this massive chapter module, we will explore the physical nature extensively.

Physical Nature & Particulate Theory

Matter is Made of Particles

For a long time, two schools of thought prevailed regarding the nature of matter. One school believed matter to be continuous like a block of wood, whereas the other thought that matter was made up of particles like sand. Let us perform an activity to decide about the nature of matter.

Activity 1.1: Dissolving Salt

Take a 100 mL beaker. Fill half the beaker with water and mark the level. Dissolve some salt/sugar with a glass rod. Observe any change in water level. Where does the salt go? Does the level change?

Conclusion: When we dissolve salt in water, the particles of salt get into the spaces between particles of water. This proves matter is particulate.

How small are these particles?

The particles of matter are very small – they are small beyond our imagination! The concept of the 'Atom' emerges from this extreme smallness.

The Potassium Permanganate Experiment: If we take just 2-3 crystals of potassium permanganate and dissolve them in 100ml of water, the water gets a deep purple color. Even if we keep diluting it (taking 10ml and adding 90ml clear water) 5 to 8 times, the water still remains colored. This single experiment demonstrates that just one tiny crystal contains millions of tiny particles that keep dividing themselves into smaller and smaller particles.

Characteristics of Particles

1

Particles of matter have space between them

In the experiments of sugar, salt, Dettol, or potassium permanganate, we saw that particles of one type of matter get evenly distributed into the spaces between particles of the other. When we make tea, coffee or lemonade, the same thing happens. This indicates that there is enough space between particles of matter, scientifically known as intermolecular or interatomic space.

2

Particles of matter are continuously moving

If you put an unlit incense stick in a corner, you have to go close to smell it. But light it, and the smell reaches you from meters away. Why? Because particles possess Kinetic Energy. As temperature rises, particles move faster. We can say that with an increase in temperature, the kinetic energy of the particles also increases.

3

Particles of matter attract each other

Try breaking an iron nail, a piece of chalk, and a rubber band. The iron nail requires immense force. This is because particles of matter have a force acting between them keeping them together. The strength of this force of attraction varies from one kind of matter to another. This is known as the intermolecular force of attraction.

Kinetic Theory of Matter

Deep Dive: Kinetic Theory

The kinetic theory of matter provides a microscopic explanation for macroscopic properties of matter. The fundamental postulates are:

  • All matter is composed of tiny, discrete particles (atoms, ions, or molecules).
  • These particles are in constant, random motion (translatory, rotatory, or vibratory).
  • The particles possess kinetic energy due to this motion.
  • Collisions between these particles are perfectly elastic (no net loss of kinetic energy).
  • The average kinetic energy of the particles is directly proportional to the absolute temperature.

The Phenomenon of Diffusion

The intermixing of particles of two different types of matter on their own is called diffusion. We also observe that on heating, diffusion becomes faster. Why? Because heating increases the thermal energy, which converts into kinetic energy, making the particles zip around at much higher velocities, causing rapid intermixing.

Example: The smell of hot sizzling food reaches you several meters away, but to get the smell from cold food you have to go close.

Intermolecular Forces (JEE Deep Dive)

Van der Waals Forces & Beyond

In competitive physics and chemistry, the "force of attraction" is broken down mathematically and conceptually. Intermolecular forces are the forces of attraction and repulsion between interacting particles (atoms and molecules). This term does not include the electrostatic forces that exist between the two oppositely charged ions and the forces that hold atoms of a molecule together i.e., covalent bonds.

1. Dispersion / London Forces

Atoms and nonpolar molecules are electrically symmetrical. However, a momentary dipole can be created due to uneven electron distribution. This induces a dipole in a neighboring atom. These are the weakest forces.

2. Dipole-Dipole Forces

Act between molecules possessing permanent dipole moments (like HCl). The ends of the dipoles possess "partial charges". Stronger than London forces.

3. Dipole-Induced Dipole

Exists between a polar molecule having a permanent dipole and a non-polar molecule lacking a permanent dipole. The permanent dipole induces a dipole on the electrically neutral molecule.

4. Hydrogen Bonding

A special, very strong type of dipole-dipole interaction. Occurs only when H is covalently bonded to highly electronegative atoms (F, O, N). This is why water has an exceptionally high boiling point.

The Solid State

Rigidity & Structure

Solids are characterized by strong intermolecular forces that keep the constituent particles in fixed positions. They can only oscillate about their mean positions.

Properties of Solids:

  • Have a definite shape, distinct boundaries, and fixed volumes.
  • Have negligible compressibility.
  • Solids have a tendency to maintain their shape when subjected to outside force (Rigidity).
  • Solids may break under force but it is difficult to change their shape.
  • Density of solids is generally high.

Addressing the Exceptions

Rubber Band: It changes shape under force and regains the same shape when the force is removed. If excessive force is applied, it breaks. It is a solid.
Salt & Sugar: When kept in different jars, they take the shape of the jar. However, the shape of each individual crystal remains fixed.
Sponge: It is a solid yet we are able to compress it. Why? It has minute holes, in which air is trapped. When we press it, the air is expelled out.

The Liquid State

Fluidity & Flow

In liquids, intermolecular forces are strong enough to keep particles bound together in a definite volume, but weak enough to allow them to slide past one another.

Properties of Liquids:

  • Liquids have no fixed shape but have a fixed volume.
  • They take up the shape of the container in which they are kept.
  • Liquids flow and change shape, so they are not rigid but can be called fluid.
  • Liquids are almost incompressible (hydraulics rely on this).
  • Rate of diffusion is higher than that of solids.

Biological Importance of Diffusion in Liquids

Solids and liquids can diffuse into liquids. The gases from the atmosphere diffuse and dissolve in water. These gases, especially oxygen and carbon dioxide, are essential for the survival of aquatic animals and plants. All living creatures need to breathe for survival. Aquatic animals can breathe under water due to the presence of dissolved oxygen in water.

The Gaseous State

High Compressibility & Random Motion

Gases have the weakest intermolecular forces. The particles are far apart and move randomly at very high speeds in all directions.

Properties of Gases:

  • Neither fixed shape nor fixed volume.
  • Highly compressible.
  • Exert pressure on the walls of the container.
  • Lowest density among the three states.
  • Highest rate of diffusion.

Real-World Application

Because of high compressibility, large volumes of a gas can be compressed into a small cylinder and transported easily.

  • LPG: Liquefied Petroleum Gas used in homes.
  • CNG: Compressed Natural Gas used as fuel in vehicles.
  • Oxygen Cylinders: Supplied to hospitals.

The Pressure of Gas

In the gaseous state, the particles move about randomly at high speed. Due to this random movement, the particles hit each other and also the walls of the container. The pressure exerted by the gas is because of this force exerted by gas particles per unit area on the walls of the container.

Plasma & Bose-Einstein Condensate

While solid, liquid, and gas are the three common states, modern scientists talk about five states of matter.

The 4th State: Plasma

The state consists of super energetic and super excited particles. These particles are in the form of ionized gases.

  • The fluorescent tube and neon sign bulbs consist of plasma.
  • Inside a neon sign bulb there is neon gas and inside a fluorescent tube there is helium gas or some other gas.
  • The gas gets ionized, that is, gets charged when electrical energy flows through it. This charging up creates a plasma glowing inside the tube or bulb.
  • The sun and the stars glow because of the presence of plasma in them. The plasma is created in stars because of very high temperature.

The 5th State: BEC

In 1920, Indian physicist Satyendra Nath Bose made some calculations for a fifth state of matter. Building on his calculations, Albert Einstein predicted a new state of matter – the Bose-Einstein Condensate (BEC).

How is it formed? The BEC is formed by cooling a gas of extremely low density, about one-hundred-thousandth the density of normal air, to super low temperatures (near absolute zero, 0 Kelvin or -273.15°C). At this point, the atoms clump together and enter the same energy state, acting as a single quantum "super-atom."

Change of State: Temperature Effects

When we heat a solid, the kinetic energy of its particles increases. Due to the increase in kinetic energy, the particles start vibrating with greater speed. The energy supplied by heat overcomes the forces of attraction between the particles. The particles leave their fixed positions and start moving more freely. A stage is reached when the solid melts and is converted to a liquid. The minimum temperature at which a solid melts to become a liquid at the atmospheric pressure is called its melting point. (e.g., Ice melts at 273.15 K).

Latent Heat (Hidden Heat)

The Mystery of Constant Temperature

During the melting process, once the melting point is reached, the temperature does not change until all the ice melts. Where does the heat energy go? This heat gets used up in changing the state by overcoming the forces of attraction between the particles. As this heat energy is absorbed by ice without showing any rise in temperature, it is considered that it gets hidden into the contents of the beaker and is known as the latent heat.

Latent Heat of Fusion: The amount of heat energy required to change 1 kg of a solid into a liquid at atmospheric pressure at its melting point.

Latent Heat of Vaporization: Heat required to change 1 kg liquid to gas at its boiling point.

Sublimation & Effect of Pressure

Skipping the Liquid Phase

A change of state directly from solid to gas without changing into liquid state is called sublimation and the direct change of gas to solid without changing into liquid is called deposition. Example: Camphor, Ammonium Chloride.

Effect of Change of Pressure: Applying pressure and reducing temperature can liquefy gases. Solid carbon dioxide (CO2) is stored under high pressure. Solid CO2 gets converted directly to gaseous state on decrease of pressure to 1 atmosphere without coming into liquid state. This is the reason that solid carbon dioxide is also known as dry ice.

Evaporation & Cooling Effects

A Surface Phenomenon

Do we always need to heat or change pressure for changing the state of matter? Water, when left uncovered, slowly changes into vapour. Wet clothes dry up. What happens? Particles of matter are never at rest. At any given temperature in any gas, liquid or solid, there are particles with different amounts of kinetic energy. In the case of liquids, a small fraction of particles at the surface, having higher kinetic energy, is able to break away from the forces of attraction of other particles and gets converted into vapour. This is Evaporation.

Why does it cause cooling?

In an open vessel, the liquid keeps on evaporating. The particles of liquid absorb energy from the surrounding to regain the energy lost during evaporation. This absorption of energy from the surroundings makes the surroundings cold. Example: Pouring acetone (nail polish remover) on your palm.
